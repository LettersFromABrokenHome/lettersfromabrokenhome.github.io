Dual Flame

Written on 11/27/2025

Uploaded on 2/25/2026



This speech, I wrote for one of my friends, who was afraid to ask someone out. It served more as a reflection for myself since I never sent it to them. Not because I didn't feel they needed to hear it, but because there was never a reason to.






There's a belief out there called the invisible string theory. It says that everyone out there has a soul mate, a twin flame, someone for them chosen by the stars. And the invisible string theory says that these two people have a string tied around their fingers that they cannot see. The string bends, twists, but cannot break. And every day it shortens. Slowly bringing the twin flames together. You may be closer to your fame than you think. An integral part of this belief is you don't know who your other half is. You have to find out for yourself. And since you're never told, the only way to find the other end of the string is through trial and error. 


That's one thing Hasbro got right. Life is a game. But it's like no game you've played before. It's got no instruction manual in its case. No tutorial level when you begin. And there's no walk-throughs online, because nobody has ever beaten the game before. Yes, trillions of people have made it through life, but your game is different than your parents'. No game is the same, so no walk-through for your play-through can exist. You're on your own playing through each level. The puzzle game, that is life, has infinitely many solutions for each chamber. And everyone going through finds their own path. Your pass through a stage is most certainly different than your buddie's. And that's okay, because if we all beat the game the same way it wouldn't be fun or entertaining.


You can't beat life by sitting and waiting. You act. You make a move, learn from your move, and move again. Each time you do something its an experience you learn from. An experience that betters you. You learn how to play the game, your way. Through trial and error you find a solution for each level until you meet The Boss. The Boss, who curated the game, judges your play-through. Then says "Game Over". You should never concede early. Because even though you may feel softlocked, believe me, you're not. There's always another solution: The Gamemaster will bail you out.


How does this game digression tie back to the twin flame theory? It's a perfect analogy for the only way to find your flame. You need to play the game of life. Playing through your twin flame will be revealed. But if you don't play, you don't make that move, you'll never learn.


It takes nothing to do any old move, but it takes courage to do something you're afraid to do. Cowards take the easy path. William Shakesphere said it best; "Cowards die many times before their deaths; The valiant never taste of death but once." (Julius Cesar, 2.2.34-5). If you never try, you'll never succeed.


A common expression used so many times its tiring, but out of fear of sounding like a generic orator I use it. You miss one hundred percent of the shots you don't take. If you never act on anything, you'll never find what you want to learn.


This ties back to the main point I'm trying so desperately to make; never let fear stop you. From trying something new, or more to the point of this discussion, stop you from asking someone out. If you like them, take a chance. Shoot your shot. You may miss, and that's okay. Even the best archers in the world miss the target from time to time. We're no William Tell or Katniss Everdeen. We can't always split another arrow in half. But we learn from our failure, and ready our bow again, because that shot wasn't yours. You'll have a shot, you'll land bullseye. But if you never let go of your arrow, you'll never know if that shot is yours.


No matter what happens, remember, there's still a flame out there burning. Waiting for you. Find it. So you two can set the world ablaze.
