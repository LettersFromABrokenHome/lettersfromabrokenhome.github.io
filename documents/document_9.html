The Best Book

Written on 03/05/2026

Uploaded on 03/05/2026

The Bible is the best book ever written. No argument to it.

It’s such a pity to see how people work and operate. A person does something wrong or misses church. They then say they’re not worthy for God. That they’re too great of a sinner to enter His home. That’s when things get bad. They fall victim to a cycle of not being worthy. They want to return to Him but don’t feel good enough to be blessed by His presence. Or they don’t know where to start.

But that’s the beauty of God, and the faith. The book that binds our faith. Those pivotal seventy-three books are what lays the groundwork for our religion. The words we live by. They tell us God is not only for the super rich, or the super poor. He is not exclusive to the sinners, or to the sinless. God is for everyone. We learn this through the four Gospels of the Holy Text.

In the Gospels we’re introduced to a character who is the cornerstone of the Catholic faith. His name is Jesus. Jesus was the physical human embodiment of God. As written in the Gospel of John;

 “The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the

glory of the one and only Son, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth.”[1]

Jesus is God. Jesus is God humbling Himself to walk amongst a land of sin and evil. Jesus is God’s messenger to His people, the Israelites. What love that must be. To send your only son to die for people who have hurt you for millenia. And that’s the point. God made Himself man, to show us how to live, to guide us.

This draws me back to my opening point. People don’t go to Church because they feel unworthy. Unworthy for God. But, no one is unworthy for God.

How do I mean this? How can I possibly intend to say that no person to roam this earth isn’t worthy of His presence. I’m not making anything up. This was told to us in the Bible. The text many find too scary to read or pick up. Looking at this and reading and learning, do we come to see how God wants to be for all.

We also see a powerful story through Jesus. The abolition of old rules, traditions, and institution of the new commandments. Please stick with me through this next part, it will be a tiring information dump that is vital for the rest going forward.

We see in the Book of Exodus, the second book of the Bible, God tells the Israelites to do this ritual yearly to remember how He has delivered them. A young unblemished lamb is to live with them for two weeks. They are then to slaughter the lamb and not a bone of its body is to be broken. Its blood is to then be taken and smeared on the doorposts with a branch made of hyssop. They then are to eat the cooked meat of the lamb with unleavened bread.[2] This ritual is called Passover.

Next I will take you to Leviticus Chapters 11-16. These are found in the Old Testament, before Jesus enters the scene. This book follows Exodus, which is when Moses led the Israelites out of Egypt. It’s a terribly long story, but the Israelites fall into sin and are banished from the Promised Land for 40 years. God eventually gives Moses rules about who is allowed to enter their form of a temple, this occurs in the aforementioned chapters. The Israelites are forbidden to enter if they’re unclean. Being unclean was caused by touching a dead body, skin disease, touching bodily discharge, childbirth, and touching unclean animals.

That was my massive backstory dump. Now we’ll jump ahead several millenia. The Jewish people are now in the Promised Land, and have been for quite some time. We’ll transition into the New Testament, to witness the birth of Christ.

This story is found in Luke Chapter 2, and Matthew’s first and second chapters. It’s told slightly differently in both. But the core idea is the same. The infant child is born of a virgin woman, in a manger. A stable, where animals slept, ate, and did other things. We see the pure Jesus born in a lowly place, amongst animals. A place, that following the Jewish rules of cleanliness, would have made Him impure. In fact, Mary, following these traditions, was extremely impure, and unfit for the Lord’s presence. She was lying amongst animal excrement, animals (some of which were the “unclean” animals laid out in Leviticus), and had just given birth, and was presumably covered in a bit of bodily discharge. So she checked three of  the five “unclean” boxes. But yet, still found herself in the presence of God.

We see here, in this moment, God’s first move at abolishing the old commandments. Which were created to guide the newly freed people of Israel on a path of righteousness. God puts Mary in His presence at the state of her most uncleanliness. He enters when she is in that state. This was His silent destruction of those rules.

Here ties back in the starting concept, being unworthy for His presence. We see three groups kneel before the child. The animals, the shepherds, and the Magi. Let’s go with the animals in the manger first. This has significance. This shows that even the animals recognize Jesus as God. The loveliest of creatures on this planet recognize the power in this infant child. The other groups tie further back into my thesis. The shepherds were the lowest of low, and they were the first group of people God told about the Christ child’s presence. The most looked down upon in society, God came to first.

The last group of people to see the child were the Magi, wisemen, or three kings. All the same group, just different names. These were wealthy smart scholars, who studied the stars, and found their way to Jesus. They then gave him three gifts of frankincense, gold, and myrrh. Symbolic of Jesus’s death and kingship. But these high-societal men saw Jesus as the king, and prostrated themselves before Him.

Jesus’ birth is God saying “My Son is the Lord of all. Not exclusively to the rich, or to the poor. To the sinless or the sinners. He’s the Lord and Savior of all humanity.”.

But God knows that people take a lot to be convinced, more on that later. So he reaffirms that everyone deserves to be in His presence.

Throughout the Gospels we see more of this. Jesus healing, and touching a leper[3]. Abolition of another of the unclean rules from Leviticus. We see the fish and bread brought forth in the famous Feeding of the 5,000 story offered by a kid.[4] One of the lowliest of the people there.

We see a Roman centurion, one who would have been an enemy of Jesus and his people, kneel at Jesus’ feet and beg for His help to cure his deathly ill servant. Saying, “Lord, don’t trouble yourself, for I do not deserve to have you come under my roof. That is why I did not even consider myself worthy to come to you. But say the word, and my servant will be healed.”[5]. Jesus then turned and proclaimed that He had not seen more faith than in this man.[6]


God proves Himself to be for everyone. He further pushes this home at the Death of His son. Jesus Christ was crucified. He died the death of a criminal. The lowest of low for humanity. Throughout these examples and so many more does God show Jesus to be for the lowest criminal, to the wisest scholar, and everyone in between.

Now to tie a loose end remaining from earlier. God knows that people won’t believe and will not. He faces the Pharisees with Jesus’ miracles so many times. We see this in the healing of the man born blind.[7] The Pharisees are presented with a man Jesus gave sight to, are given testimonials of his blindness, and yet refuse to believe in the miracle. Here Jesus says something that calls back to Exodus, in it we see the phrase “Pharaoh’s heart hardened”[8] every time he was presented with a miracle from Moses. Jesus here calls back to that saying “For judgment I have come into this world, so that the blind will see and those who see will become blind.”[9]. Here Jesus is saying blindness instead of a hardened heart, but he carries the same message still. Those who are open to God will see His power.

We see another moment of God presenting a miracle to the skeptics and still see them lacking faith. This is in the garden of Gethsemane, hours before Jesus’ death. One of the disciples cuts off a soldier's ear. Jesus then heals the man’s ear. The Pharisees see this and still call him a heretic.[10]

There is one last loose end for me to tie up. That, of course, is Passover. See Jesus was the offering of the final Passover lamb. He was an unblemished male, slaughtered with his blood smeared upon a frame. He was offered wine upon a branch of hyssop.[11]

Passover was the symbol of God’s covenant with the Israelites. Jesus was His final lamb. The formation of the new covenant.

There is so much more in the Bible to unpack and unravel, and I promise soon I will tell you so much more in the time to come. Here I have given two major themes of the Holy Text for you to decipher and meditate on this season of Lent. I wish you well in your journey as God has brought you well.


God Bless,
